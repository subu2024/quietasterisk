_Let me say this first_: I wasn’t looking for love.

Love requires exposure. And exposure has never ended well for me.

The last time I let someone close, she left while I was still trying to fix the argument. I remember the sound of the zipper on her suitcase more clearly than her goodbye. Since then, I’ve preferred books — they close when you close them.

Predictable. Contained. Safe.

Then there was Meera.

---

We met at the Barnes & Noble in Mosaic.

I was hovering between _Sapiens_ and _The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F_ when I noticed her in Philosophy, head tilted, lips moving slightly as she read spines like they were secrets.

She wasn’t trying to be seen.

Loose sweater. Hair tied back in a way that suggested practicality, not performance. No makeup I could detect. She had the kind of stillness that made noise around her feel unnecessary.

I stared too long.

She looked up. Direct. Unapologetic.

“Do I look like an exhibit?”

I blinked. “No. I mean—maybe? I wasn’t—”

“Relax,” she said, almost smiling. “You just look like you’re deciding whether to judge me or join me.”

That startled a laugh out of me.

“Join you in what?”

“Questioning everything,” she said, holding up a book. “Or pretending we understand it.”

She misquoted the author a second later, caught herself, and muttered, “That’s not what he meant at all. See? Fraud.”

And just like that, she wasn’t intimidating. She was human.

---

We kept running into each other.

Café line. Parking lot. Same aisle. Too consistent to be entirely accidental.

By the third encounter she said, “You again? Are you assigned to this section?”

“I haunt bookstores,” I said. “People complicate things.”

She snorted. “Books complicate things. They just do it politely.”

There was something about the way she argued — quick, alive, slightly chaotic. Not polished. Not rehearsed. When she got animated, she talked with her hands and knocked over a display of bookmarks. She apologized to the shelf.

That’s when I knew I was in trouble.

---

The bar down the street became ours.

It smelled like wood polish and stories no one finished. She ordered gin and tonic. I ordered whiskey, though I usually avoided it. It felt like stepping slightly outside myself.

“So,” she said one night, “what are you afraid of?”

I deflected. “That’s subtle.”

“Answer anyway.”

I studied the condensation on my glass. “Investing in something that leaves.”

She didn’t interrupt. That alone made my throat tighten.

“I don’t want to be fixed,” I said. “I don’t want to fix anyone either. I just want honesty.”

She nodded slowly. “Good. Because I’m unfinished. And sometimes dramatic. And occasionally wrong.”

“Occasionally?”

“Frequently,” she corrected. “But I don’t lie. Not even to make things easier.”

The word easier lingered between us.

Her phone buzzed.

She glanced down. A name. She flipped it face-down a little too quickly.

“Ex,” she said. “He thinks persistence equals romance.”

“Does it?” I asked.

“No,” she said. Then softer, “It equals noise.”

For reasons I didn’t examine, that answer mattered more than it should have.

---

Weeks blurred into rhythm.

Tuesdays in bookstores. Fridays at the bar. Sunday walks where she’d tell stories about her daughter, Maya — about unfinished science projects and dramatic eye rolls and the way she refused to eat vegetables unless renamed.

“She only eats broccoli if I call them ‘tiny trees,’” Meera confessed once, embarrassed. “I negotiate with a preteen daily. This is my life.”

It grounded her. Made her real. Made this real.

And that terrified me.

---

When she invited me to dinner, I almost said no.

Not because I didn’t want to go.

Because it meant something.

Her apartment smelled faintly of jasmine and something overcooked. Rice, as it turned out.

“I was distracted,” she said defensively, fanning smoke away. “This never happens.”

“Lies,” a voice called from the hallway.

Maya.

Eleven. Observant. Surgical.

She looked at me for exactly three seconds before asking, “Are you my mom’s boyfriend?”

My pulse tripped over itself.

“Absolutely not,” Meera said too quickly.

Maya narrowed her eyes. “You make her laugh different.”

Different.

Not more. Not louder.

Different.

I didn’t know why that nearly undid me.

Later, as I helped clear plates, I noticed a drawing on the fridge. Three stick figures. One taller, unfinished in pencil.

I didn’t ask about it.

---

After Maya went to her room, the apartment felt quieter.

Meera leaned against the counter, arms crossed, watching me like she was trying to solve something.

“She likes you,” she said.

“That’s dangerous.”

A small smile. “We’re a package deal.”

I nodded.

In the past, that sentence would’ve sent me running. Complexity. Attachment. Stakes.

Instead, I stayed where I was.

---

Later, we stood by the window. City lights blinking below like coded messages.

“I’m not scared of your scars,” she said quietly.

I laughed under my breath. “You don’t even know them.”

“I don’t need the details to know they exist.”

Silence stretched.

She looked down at her wine glass. Turned it in slow circles.

“I don’t want temporary,” she said.

My chest tightened.

“I’ve done temporary. I’ve survived temporary.” She inhaled shakily. “I don’t want to survive something again.”

I didn’t speak. I couldn’t.

She met my eyes then — really met them.

“I want…” She exhaled, almost annoyed with herself. “God, this is reckless.”

“Meera—”

“I want to marry you.”

The word didn’t float.

It landed.

Heavy. Fragile. Real.

Her face flushed immediately. “I didn’t mean now. I mean— not tomorrow. I just—”

“I know,” I said.

And I did.

For years, I’d been the first to leave. Even when I stayed physically, some part of me always packed early.

But standing there, I realized something unsettling:

I didn’t want an exit.

“I’m scared,” I admitted.

“Me too,” she said.

That helped more than reassurance would have.

“You deserve someone who stays,” she whispered.

The old instinct — retreat, deflect, joke — flickered.

Then it faded.

“I’m still here,” I said.

And for the first time in a long time, that wasn’t temporary.

I kissed her — not because it was cinematic, not because the city blurred, but because it felt like agreement. Like stepping forward instead of bracing backward.

---

I wasn’t looking for love.

I was looking for control. For something that couldn’t walk away while I was mid-sentence.

But love didn’t arrive as certainty.

It arrived as a woman who burns rice, misquotes philosophers, negotiates with a child over broccoli, and says reckless things out loud.

And instead of running—

I stayed.
